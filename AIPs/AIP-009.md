## Abstract

AIP-009 proposes a decentralized manufacturing protocol for Arrow to enable global production of its designs without centralized entities. Orders are fulfilled by bonded ecosystem manufacturers via an Ethereum-based dApp shop that routes customer orders based on geography, price, and other factors. The on-chain catalog lists products with descriptions, per-unit $ARROW bond requirements, and DAO commissions varying by design investment (e.g., high for Quiver drones, low for commodities). Manufacturers post slashable $ARROW bonds as collateral for quality and compliance, earning proportional rewards from a periodic DAO-authorized pool based on sales volume; unstaking incurs a 28-day cooldown. The DAO governs by onboarding manufacturers via NFTs, resolving disputes through slashing, managing the catalog, and funding protocol development to ensure trust-minimized, community-driven hardware production aligned with Arrow's mission.

## Motivation

In the past, Arrow has struggled with inefficiencies and coordination challenges when we have attempted aircraft builds and other real-world operations. AIP-009 proposes a protocol design that would allow for Arrow’s designs to be manufactured and sold in the real world, while the DAO remains internet-native, decentralized, and not directly tied to any physical location or regime.

AIP-009 intends to create a reasonable path for growing Arrow towards our goals set in AIP-008 while capturing value for the DAO to reinvest in future projects. Arrow’s manufacturing approach should be community-first and it should embrace our online capacity. This way, our ecosystem and community should grow as we do more manufacturing.

Most importantly, Arrow should have a manufacturing system that delivers excellent products and support to our customers, while enabling global distribution.

This AIP is intended to describe the proposed protocol at a basic level, so that the community can engage and reach consensus on whether to proceed with the concept. The details specified here in AIP-009 aren’t meant to be binding and permanent, but rather to set the general goals and development direction for the protocol. If AIP-009 is approved, the DAO should plan to follow up by funding a project to begin designing and developing the protocol in detail.

## Specification

Arrow SHALL create an on-chain protocol and associated infrastructure (Web apps, governance tools and processes, etc) to coordinate an ecosystem of manufacturers that will build, stock, and deliver Arrow’s products. We will design incentives so that the DAO, manufacturers, and customers can all associate and interact in a trust minimized way. Manufacturers MUST post a slash-able bond of $ARROW token to guarantee alignment with the DAO and assure quality to customers. The DAO will be able to onboard manufacturers globally, so that we can scale our production quickly and in a community-driven manner.

When possible, Arrow funded hardware and software SHOULD use a copyleft open source license such as GPL or CERN-OHL-S (footnote \- https://vitalik.eth.limo/general/2025/07/07/copyleft.html). Arrow MAY consider other licenses if there is a compelling reason to do so. With Arrow’s designs open, our ecosystem will allow anyone to manufacture the designs directly without the DAO being directly involved in the real-world operations. Arrow will serve the role of an ecosystem facilitator, ensuring quality, support, and availability for customers.

### Customer Interface \- The Arrow Shop

Arrow SHALL build a web shop that functions as an easy interface between customers and Arrow’s underlying protocols. Also known as a dApp, this web shop SHOULD be accessible to both crypto-native users and those unfamiliar with crypto. User orders placed through the web shop will be routed to a manufacturer through on-chain order routing.

When customers place an order on the web shop, they can expect Arrow branded product, with an identical build and level of quality (with minor manufacturer marking allowed), no matter which manufacturer ultimately fulfills their order.

Both crypto and traditional credit card payments SHALL be available to the customer.

Customers MAY select a specific manufacturer to fulfill their order, but it will default to whichever is closest geographically. Later releases of the Arrow shop SHOULD select manufacturer(s) for an order based on price, fulfillment time, shipping cost, and inventory.

### Parts Catalog

Arrow SHALL build an on-chain catalog of our parts and products that will be available via the protocol.

For each entry in the catalog, we SHALL store item names and design descriptions that are sufficient for a manufacturer to build them to specification and evaluate quality. This design description will be the reference to evaluate any potential slashing action if a manufacturer does not meet the standard. The design descriptions MAY include CAD files, finish quality examples, tolerances, test procedures, or anything else necessary to set a clear standard for the build.

Although this will primarily be referenced on-chain, it will link to off-chain data. When possible, Arrow SHOULD design the protocol to use IPFS or a similar protocol to minimize dependency on any particular host and guarantee immutability.

For each entry, the catalog SHALL also maintain variables for the $ARROW bond required for a manufacturer to ship one unit of that item per bonding period, and for the commission percentage, which will be the amount paid to the DAO for each sale of that item.

Additions, removals, and changes to the catalog SHALL place via DAO governance.

### Manufacturer Interface

Manufacturers who wish to join Arrow’s protocol MUST post an $ARROW token bond as a guarantee that they will be a good actor, and adhere to quality, timeline, and protocol rules.

In initial versions of the protocol, manufacturers MUST obtain DAO approval before joining, however the manufacturer SHOULD eventually become permissionless, requiring only a bond. The DAO MAY also set a maximum bond size for a particular manufacturer to reduce the risk of a rapid ramp up by a new manufacturer.

The DAO MAY slash a manufacturer’s bond if they harm an Arrow customer by failing to ship a product or shipping a low quality product. In this case, the slashed funds will be used to compensate the affected customer. The DAO MAY also slash a manufacturer’s bond if they actively work against the interests of Arrow, such as by selling Arrow’s products outside of our protocol.

In return for the manufacturer’s bond, Arrow will provide order flow from its customers, support with manufacturing processes, and the right to officially sell products on behalf of Arrow. Manufacturers will not be expected to do their own marketing or any other work outside of fulfilling orders to the specifications of the catalog.

Each time a manufacturer fulfills an order, the amount of $ARROW specified in the catalog for each unit of product that was sold are temporarily “used” and locked on their bond. After a predetermined amount of time, currently planned to be about a month, the tokens are released and available for withdrawal or to be used to guarantee other orders. This lockup is to allow time for a customer to receive and inspect their product, and report any issues that could result in a slashing. If all of a manufacturer’s tokens are locked up with orders, then they may not fulfill any more orders until they have more tokens available.

Manufacturers SHOULD also receive additional $ARROW token rewards on top of their stake. This may be proportional to total order volume, or targeted to specific conditions that the DAO wishes to incentivize, such as inventory in stock for certain parts.

### DAO Governance and Responsibilities

The DAO serves as the final governance and decision-making authority for the entire protocol.

Until the manufacturer role becomes permissionless, the DAO SHALL evaluate applications from potential manufacturers and issue them risk-adjusted bond limits.

The DAO SHALL generally monitor the protocol for fairness and correct any misaligned incentives or protocol abuse.

The DAO SHALL maintain the catalog. It SHALL add, remove, and edit parts as necessary. It SHALL adjust bond and commission values for entries to compensate for market conditions.

The DAO MUST review customer grievances and act as a fair arbiter between customers and manufacturers, and slash manufacturer bonds when necessary.

The DAO SHALL take responsibility for customer development, marketing, support and ultimately work to grow the protocol with more customers and manufacturers over time.

The DAO SHOULD use targeted $ARROW incentives to drive desired outcomes for protocol security and growth.

## Future Expansions

The following is a list of potential future expansions to Arrow’s manufacturing protocol that may be considered for future versions.

Third Party Arbitration \- Integrate with Kleros court or a similar external protocol to resolve disputes over slashing.

Delegated Bonding \- Allow users to bond $ARROW on behalf of another manufacturer.

Quality Score/Reputation \- Track a score for each manufacturer based on prior performance and inspections.

Permissionless Manufacturers \- Allow anyone to join as a manufacturer by posting $ARROW bond only.

Inspection Program \- Anonymously inspect products from each manufacturer. We could offer a discount on user orders if they upload a teardown.

Advanced Incentives \- We could incentivize specific quantities in inventory, or incentivize the network such that we assure that we aren’t ever dependent on a single manufacturer for a particular part.

**Examples (narrative)**

**Example 1 – Supply-Chain Assembly**  
**SkyFab** (a bonded manufacturer in Turkey) lists 500 units of the Quiver aluminum airframe in the catalog. **WingTech** (a smaller shop in the US) sees the listing, and buys 50 airframes via the Arrow shop. WingTech assembles full Quiver drones, adding motors and PCBs. A hobbyist in Seattle orders a complete devkit for $6000; the shop defaults to WingTech (closest). WingTech ships in 4 days. The sale locks 5,000 $ARROW of WingTech’s bond for 28 days. After inspection, tokens unlock; WingTech earns 300 $ARROW reward from the DAO pool. After the DAOs 20% commission, WingTech receives $4800 from the sale. SkyFab never touches the customer directly.

**Example 2 – Bond Limit**  
**DroneCo** starts with a 60,000 $ARROW bond. The catalog requires 5,000 $ARROW per Quiver drone shipped. DroneCo receives five orders in one week → 25,000 $ARROW of bond is locked (5,000 x 5). They can still fulfill 7 more orders with the remaining 35,000. The next week, DroneCo receives seven more orders and has hit their bond limit. They aren’t able to take any more orders for three weeks until the first five orders hit the rolling 28 day timer. DroneCo’s effective capacity was capped at 12 units/month until they add bond or wait for unlock. This rolling window ensures capital efficiency while protecting customers.

*Note: This assumes a rolling 28 day period per unit shipped. Actual implementation could vary depending on smart contract logic.*

**Example 3 – Slashing & Compensation**  
**FlyCheap** ships 20 Quiver drones with cracked carbon fiber tubes. Three customers file grievances with photos. The DAO reviews and governance votes to slash 50 % of the per-unit bond (5,000 × 20 × 0.5). The 10,000 $ARROW is used to send new carbon fiber tubes and a repair kit to the affected customers. The DAO publishes the case on-chain for transparency.
